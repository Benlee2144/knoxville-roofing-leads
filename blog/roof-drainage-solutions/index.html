Roof Drainage Solutions: Keeping Water Away from Your Home

Updated February 2025 • 7 min read

Water is your roof's biggest enemy. Knoxville gets an average of 47 inches of rain per year – that's a lot of water that needs to get off your roof and away from your home. Poor drainage leads to roof damage, foundation problems, basement flooding, and landscape erosion.

Here's how to make sure all that water goes where it should.

Components of a Roof Drainage System

Roof Slope

The most basic drainage element is the roof itself. Steep roofs shed water quickly; low-slope roofs need additional help. Most residential roofs in West Knoxville and Farragut have adequate slope for water shedding.

Gutters

Gutters collect water as it runs off the roof edge and direct it to downspouts. They're essential for protecting your fascia, siding, and foundation.

Downspouts

Vertical pipes that carry water from gutters down to ground level. Proper sizing and placement are critical for handling heavy rain.

Extensions and Drainage

Water leaving the downspout must be directed away from your foundation. This can involve extensions, splash blocks, underground drains, or French drains.

Gutter Types and Options

K-Style Gutters

The most common residential gutter. Named for their profile shape, they handle water well and are available in multiple sizes.

  • 5-inch: Standard for most homes
  • 6-inch: Better for large roofs or heavy rainfall

Half-Round Gutters

Traditional style often seen on historic homes. Less capacity than K-style but attractive and easier to clean.

Materials

  • Aluminum: Most popular. Lightweight, rust-free, affordable
  • Copper: Beautiful, extremely durable, expensive
  • Galvanized steel: Strong but can rust over time
  • Vinyl: Cheapest but least durable
Recommendation: For most Knoxville homes, aluminum gutters in 5 or 6-inch K-style provide the best balance of performance, durability, and cost.

Common Drainage Problems

Clogged Gutters

The most frequent issue. Leaves, pine needles, and debris block water flow, causing:

  • Water overflow onto fascia and siding
  • Water backing up under shingles
  • Gutter sagging and pulling away
  • Foundation water problems

Undersized Gutters

Gutters that can't handle heavy rain overflow during storms. This is common in homes with large roof areas or steep slopes that concentrate runoff.

Improper Slope

Gutters should slope toward downspouts (about 1/4 inch per 10 feet). Without proper slope, water pools and causes problems.

Insufficient Downspouts

Rule of thumb: one downspout for every 20-30 linear feet of gutter. Many homes don't have enough.

Poor Discharge Location

Water dumped too close to the foundation seeps into basements and damages foundations. Water should discharge at least 4-6 feet from the house.

Foundation damage is expensive. Improper drainage is the leading cause of foundation problems. Fixing drainage issues costs hundreds; fixing foundation issues costs tens of thousands.

Gutter Protection Options

If you're tired of cleaning gutters (or can't safely do it), consider guards:

Gutter Screens

Simple mesh covers that block large debris. Affordable but require occasional cleaning.

Gutter Guards

Solid covers with slots or openings that allow water in while blocking debris. More effective than screens but more expensive.

Gutter Helmets/Covers

Use surface tension to guide water into gutters while debris falls off. Most expensive but most effective.

Foam Inserts

Porous foam that sits inside gutters. Water flows through; debris sits on top. Mixed effectiveness.

No system is perfect for all debris types. Homes in Powell, Halls, and other heavily treed areas may still need occasional cleaning regardless of guard type.

Ground-Level Drainage Solutions

Downspout Extensions

Simple and affordable. Attach to downspouts to carry water further from the foundation. Available as rigid or flexible tubes.

Splash Blocks

Concrete or plastic troughs placed under downspouts to direct water away. Basic but effective for low-volume situations.

Underground Drain Lines

Buried pipes that carry water from downspouts to a distant discharge point. More attractive than surface extensions and more effective.

French Drains

Gravel-filled trenches with perforated pipes that collect and redirect groundwater. Used when surface drainage isn't sufficient.

Dry Wells

Underground chambers that collect water and allow it to percolate into the soil. Good for areas where you can't discharge to the street.

Flat Roof Drainage

Flat roofs (common on commercial buildings) need special drainage considerations:

Interior Drains

Drains installed in the roof surface that connect to interior drainage pipes. Most common on large commercial roofs.

Scuppers

Openings in the parapet wall that allow water to flow off the roof edge. Often connected to downspouts.

Tapered Insulation

Creates slope on an otherwise flat roof to direct water toward drains.

Maintenance for Good Drainage

Keep your drainage system working with regular maintenance:

Twice-Yearly Gutter Cleaning

  • Spring (after pollen and seed season)
  • Fall (after leaf drop)
  • More often if you have pine trees

Check After Major Storms

Storms can deposit debris and damage gutters. A quick inspection prevents small problems from becoming big ones.

Annual Inspection

Look for:

  • Proper gutter slope (water shouldn't pool)
  • Secure gutter attachment
  • No gaps at seams
  • Downspout connections intact
  • Discharge points directing water away

Signs of Drainage Problems

Watch for these warning signs:

  • Water stains on siding or foundation
  • Erosion near foundation
  • Basement dampness or water
  • Gutters overflowing during rain
  • Mildew or mold on exterior walls
  • Pooling water in yard after rain
  • Cracks in foundation (can indicate water damage)

The Bottom Line

Good roof drainage isn't glamorous, but it's essential for protecting your home. A few hundred dollars spent on proper gutters, guards, and ground drainage can prevent thousands in water damage repairs. Don't wait for problems to appear – be proactive about managing where the water goes.
