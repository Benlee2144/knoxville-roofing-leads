Storm Chaser Roofers: Don't Become Their Next Victim

Updated February 2025 • 7 min read

After every major storm in Knoxville and East Tennessee, they arrive. Trucks with out-of-state plates. Door knockers with rehearsed pitches. "Roofing contractors" who didn't exist in town last week and won't be here next month.

They're called storm chasers, and they've cost Tennessee homeowners millions of dollars in shoddy work, insurance fraud, and outright theft.

This is different from our general scam warning. While we cover common roofing scams elsewhere, storm chasers are a specific breed that descends on communities after severe weather. They exploit the urgency of the situation and homeowners' desperation to get repairs done.

What Is a Storm Chaser Roofer?

Storm chasers are roofing crews that travel from disaster to disaster, following severe weather events. They typically:

  • Show up within days of a major storm
  • Have no local presence or reputation
  • Use high-pressure sales tactics
  • Promise to handle your insurance claim
  • Do substandard work (or no work at all)
  • Leave town before problems surface

They prey on homeowners in West Knoxville, Farragut, Powell, and throughout Knox County every time we get significant hail or wind damage.

Red Flags to Watch For

1. They Knock on Your Door

Legitimate contractors don't canvas neighborhoods looking for work. They have enough business from referrals and their reputation. If someone shows up uninvited after a storm, be very cautious.

2. Out-of-State Plates

Check their truck. Out-of-state license plates (especially from states known for roofing fraud like Florida or Texas) are a major warning sign.

3. They Offer to Handle Everything with Your Insurance

"Just sign this, and we'll deal with your insurance company." This is often a setup for insurance fraud, inflated claims, or work that doesn't match what was claimed.

4. Pressure to Sign Today

"We have crews available right now, but only if you sign today." Legitimate contractors understand you need time to get multiple quotes and make decisions.

5. Large Upfront Payment Required

"We need 50% down to order materials." Then they disappear, or they do partial work and demand more money.

6. No Physical Business Address

They have a website (maybe), business cards, but no actual office you can visit. Google their address – is it real?

7. They Claim to Find Damage You Didn't Know About

"I was just driving by and noticed your roof has major damage." Be especially suspicious if they offer to climb up and inspect for free.

The Storm Chaser Playbook

Here's how the typical scam works:

  1. First contact: They knock on your door or leave a flyer within days of a storm
  2. Free inspection: They offer to check your roof at no cost
  3. Discovery: They "find" extensive damage (sometimes creating it themselves)
  4. Insurance promise: They offer to work directly with your insurance company
  5. Contract signing: They push for immediate commitment with high-pressure tactics
  6. Upfront payment: They collect a large deposit before starting work
  7. Substandard work: They do quick, cheap repairs that won't last
  8. Exit: They leave town before you realize the problems

The Damage They Leave Behind

Storm chaser victims often end up with:

  • Work that fails within months
  • Voided manufacturer warranties
  • Code violations that cause problems when selling
  • Insurance claims that are denied or lead to dropped coverage
  • No recourse because the company can't be found
  • The need to pay again to have the work done properly

How to Protect Yourself

After a Storm

  1. Don't panic. You have time to make a good decision
  2. Document damage yourself with photos and video before anyone touches your roof
  3. Contact your insurance company directly to file a claim
  4. Get multiple quotes from established local contractors
  5. Verify credentials – license, insurance, local references

When Vetting Contractors

  • Check their Tennessee contractor's license at verify.tn.gov
  • Verify insurance by calling the insurance company directly
  • Look for consistent local reviews over multiple years
  • Ask for local references you can actually contact
  • Verify their physical address exists
  • Check Better Business Bureau for complaints

Contract Must-Haves

  • Full company name and physical address
  • Contractor's license number
  • Detailed scope of work
  • Materials to be used (brand, model)
  • Payment schedule (no more than 30% upfront)
  • Timeline for completion
  • Warranty information

What If You've Already Hired One?

If you realize you've hired a storm chaser:

  1. Stop payment if possible (credit card dispute, stop check)
  2. Document everything – photos, contracts, communications
  3. Don't let them on your property if work hasn't started
  4. Contact a lawyer if they've taken money and not performed
  5. File complaints with the Tennessee Board for Licensing Contractors, BBB, and state attorney general
  6. Report to police if they've committed fraud or theft

Legitimate Contractors Exist

Don't let fear of storm chasers keep you from getting necessary repairs. Reputable contractors in Maryville, Oak Ridge, Sevierville, and throughout the Knoxville area do exist and do great work.

The key differences:

  • They don't knock on doors
  • They have years of local history
  • They don't pressure you to sign immediately
  • They work with your insurance properly
  • They have verifiable references and reviews
  • They'll be here next year when you need warranty service

Report Storm Chasers

If you encounter storm chasers in your neighborhood, report them:

  • Tennessee Board for Licensing Contractors: (615) 741-8307
  • Better Business Bureau
  • Local police (especially for aggressive behavior)
  • Your neighborhood watch or HOA

Spreading the word helps protect your neighbors.
