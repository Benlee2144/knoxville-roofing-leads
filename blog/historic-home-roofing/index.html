Roofing for Historic Homes in Knoxville

Updated February 2025 • 8 min read

Knoxville has beautiful historic neighborhoods – from the grand Victorian homes of Fourth and Gill to the Craftsman bungalows of Old North Knoxville. If you own one of these treasures, roofing decisions require extra consideration. The wrong choice can damage your home's character, reduce its value, and potentially violate local preservation guidelines.

Understanding Historic Designations

Before planning any roofing work, understand your home's status:

National Register of Historic Places

Homes and districts listed on the National Register have documented historical significance. While listing doesn't restrict what you can do (unless you use federal money or permits), it often indicates local oversight exists.

Local Historic Overlay (H-1)

Knoxville's Historic Zoning Overlay districts DO restrict exterior changes. If your home is in one of these districts, you'll need approval from the Historic Zoning Commission before changing your roof.

Historic Districts in Knoxville Include:

  • Fourth and Gill
  • Old North Knoxville
  • Fort Sanders
  • Mechanicsville
  • Park City
  • Portions of South Knoxville
Check before you proceed. Contact the Knoxville Historic Zoning Commission (865-215-2500) to determine if your property is in a regulated district and what approvals you may need.

Original Roofing Materials by Era

Understanding what your home originally had helps guide appropriate replacement choices:

Pre-1900 Victorian Era

  • Wood shingles or shakes
  • Slate
  • Metal (standing seam tin)
  • Clay tile (less common locally)

1900-1940 Craftsman/Bungalow Era

  • Wood shingles
  • Early asphalt shingles
  • Slate
  • Metal roofing

1940-1960 Post-War Homes

  • Asphalt shingles (3-tab)
  • Built-up roofing (flat sections)
  • Metal on certain styles

Appropriate Replacement Options

When Matching Original Material

Slate: If your historic home has slate, you have several options:

  • Repair existing: Slate can last 100+ years; individual broken slates can be replaced
  • Replace with new slate: Expensive ($15-30/sq ft installed) but authentic
  • Synthetic slate: Modern composites that look like slate at lower cost

Wood shingles/shakes:

  • Cedar shingles: Traditional choice, 25-30 year lifespan
  • Synthetic wood: Composite products that mimic wood appearance
  • Note: Wood may require fire treatment in some areas

Metal roofing:

  • Standing seam: Period-appropriate for many historic homes
  • Terne or terne-coated: Traditional material, still available
  • Painted steel: Modern alternative with historic appearance

When Using Modern Materials

If your budget or preferences point to modern materials, choose wisely:

Architectural shingles: Can work on many historic homes if you choose a style and color that complements the architecture. Avoid ultra-modern patterns.

Synthetic products: High-quality synthetic slate, shake, and tile products can provide authentic appearance at lower cost and weight.

Key principle: The goal is maintaining the home's historic character and visual integrity. Sometimes that means original materials; sometimes appropriate modern alternatives work fine.

Special Considerations for Older Homes

Structural Capacity

Older roof structures may not support heavy materials like slate or tile if they weren't originally designed for them. Have a structural assessment done before changing to heavier materials.

Ventilation

Historic homes often have minimal or no attic ventilation. Modern roofing practices may require adding ventilation, which must be done sensitively to avoid damaging historic fabric.

Roof Complexity

Victorian and other historic styles often have complex rooflines with multiple valleys, dormers, and decorative elements. These require skilled installation and may affect material choice.

Existing Damage

Older homes may have underlying damage from past leaks, poor repairs, or deferred maintenance. Budget for potential deck replacement and structural repairs.

Working with Historic Zoning

If your home is in a regulated historic district:

  1. Contact the Historic Zoning office early – before getting quotes or signing contracts
  2. Get a Certificate of Appropriateness before starting work
  3. Submit your plans showing proposed materials, colors, and methods
  4. Attend the commission meeting if required
  5. Follow approval conditions exactly

The process typically takes 2-4 weeks. Don't start work without approval – you may be required to remove inappropriate materials at your expense.

Choosing a Contractor

Not every roofer is suited for historic home work. Look for:

Experience with Historic Properties

  • Ask specifically about historic home projects
  • Request photos and references from similar work
  • Verify they understand preservation principles

Knowledge of Materials

  • Experience with slate, wood, metal, or other historic materials
  • Understanding of compatible modern alternatives
  • Ability to match existing materials for repairs

Familiarity with Local Requirements

  • Has worked in Knoxville's historic districts before
  • Understands the Certificate of Appropriateness process
  • Can help navigate approval requirements

Cost Considerations

Historic home roofing often costs more than standard residential work due to:

  • Specialized materials
  • More complex installation
  • Skilled labor requirements
  • Approval and permitting processes
  • Potential structural work

Budget 20-50% more than standard roofing costs. For accurate estimates, get quotes from contractors experienced with historic properties.

Tax Credits and Incentives

Some financial help may be available:

Federal Historic Tax Credit

20% credit for substantial rehabilitation of certified historic structures – primarily for income-producing properties, but worth investigating.

Tennessee Historic Tax Credit

State-level credit that may apply to residential properties in certain circumstances.

Local Programs

Some historic districts have grant or loan programs for preservation work. Contact Knoxville's Historic Zoning office for current offerings.

Neighborhoods with Historic Homes

While formal historic districts have the most oversight, many older homes exist throughout Bearden, West Knoxville, and other established areas. Even without formal designation, maintaining period-appropriate roofing protects your home's character and value.

The Bottom Line

Owning a historic home means being a steward of Knoxville's architectural heritage. Take roofing decisions seriously – choose appropriate materials, work with experienced contractors, and respect any preservation requirements. The extra effort pays off in a home that maintains its historic integrity for future generations.
